What else can masks be used for besides throwing them away?

Sad but true, we can't live without masks :(

Going back to the beginning of 2020, if someone told you to wear a mask for another two years, you would probably think that was an alarmist. Unexpectedly, two years have passed, masks are still a necessity for our lives, and we can’t get rid of them in a foreseeable future.

How many masks have humans made in the past two years? It doesn’t take much thinking to know it’s an astronomical figure. But the most terrifying thing is, where did they all go?

Small masks have become a big problem

There is a great concern over environmental pollution caused by improper disposal of face masks in Malaysia, especially since the government imposed a mandatory face mask law in the country areas and onboard public transport.


Images of used face masks littered in parks and public grounds have got criticisms and concerns on social media. Several areas in Klang alley also saw many face masks discarded irresponsibly. (Woman) said the littered face masks may pose health risks to sanitation workers. (Man) said littering shows the lack of discipline among Malaysians.

It takes about 400-500 years for a mask to degrade completely, probably spanning the length of time from the end of the Middle Ages to the present, not only polluting the sea, but also threatening the lives of animals.

Penguins, seabirds and marine creatures who want to feast on their meals treat the masks as sea fish or jellyfish by mistake, and eventually block the esophagus; the mask straps become natural traps, entangling the feet, wings and snouts of the animals, making them unable to function normally, even dead.


To make matters worse, these microplastics eaten by animals will eventually circulate through the food chain and be eaten by humans ourselves.

Masks, masks, we are really in a dilemma. Besides throwing them away, what else can we do with discarded masks?

Asyraf noted that the social media can help play a role in solving the littering issue. Another concerned citizen said he hopes the prices of facemasks can be lowered so that those who can’t afford them would not recycle them. The government head on Thursday said the ceiling price for face masks would be further reduced from 1.20 rm in order to lessen the public’s burden. Meanwhile (officer) is seeking a solution to dispose of 1 million single use face masks thrown daily. State environment committee chairman said (officer) is also embarking on a project to encourage more people to use reusable face masks.

1500 masks can make a stool?

Do you believe that the past life of this “frigid” chair was a pile of masks?

Have you ever thought that maybe those masks that were supposed to be thrown away can actually be turned into 🪑, 🛋, 🧥, 🔋, or even used for building a house 🧱.

The main material of the mask is polypropylene, which in layman’s terms is plastic.


These discarded masks were collected by interior designer Tobia Zambotti on the street. It takes more than 10,000 masks to make a sofa. However, it seems that it is not difficult to collect these “raw materials”.

Being sterilized, they are stuffed into sofas and jackets. Zambotti named his works “Couch-19” and “Coat-19” respectively.


Another Korean designer, Haneul Kim, has also tried turning masks into furniture, only more hardcore.



He took this project as his graduation project and named it “Stack and Stack (In Pandemic, 2020)”.

In 2021, the bench evolved into a back chair.

Haneul Kim’s idea is common in mask recycling. This way, masks exceed restrictions and become a high-quality material. While the masks look flimsy, the new material is strong and sturdy.

Entrepreneur Binish Desai, nicknamed “Indian Recycler”, installed ecological boxes in hospitals, restaurants and other public places, sterilized and crushed the collected masks and other PPEs to turn into the new brick “P-Block 2.0”.


The Moscow State Institute of Steel and Alloys in Russia announced that the recovered masks can be soaked in graphene ink to make a new type of battery with an energy density close to that of a lithium battery.

A technology company in Taiwan made nearly 40,000 power banks from recycled masks and gave them to employees. Each has a unique shape and pattern.

Taking good care of masks is a philosophy

In addition to solving the pollution of masks from a perspective of recycling, there are people who have taken a different approach: why can’t we invent masks that do not cause pollution from the beginning?

The Plastique Fantastique studio from Berlin has launched a transparent helmet that is both sunscreen and breathable.

We don’t know how long we will have to coexist with the epidemic, nor can we predict how long it will take for these new mask technologies to be popularized, so that every mask has its place.

But it’s not that we can’t do anything, at least we can learn to throw away masks properly first. wikiHow has published a “Guide to Losing Masks”:


One thing wikiHow doesn’t mention is that we can cut the mask straps off before littering so they don’t get tangled up with animals.

Maybe we will eventually usher in a life where we don’t need to wear masks to go out, but before that, this little friend who gets along with us day and night deserves to be taken more seriously.
